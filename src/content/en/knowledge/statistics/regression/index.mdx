# Linear regression

<MdxCard title="Definition">
**Linear regression** models the relationship between a target variable <MathInline>{"y"}</MathInline> and explanatory variables <MathInline>{"x_1, \\ldots, x_p"}</MathInline> through a linear combination of coefficients.

In the multiple case:

<MathBlock>{"y = X\\beta + \\varepsilon"}</MathBlock>

where <MathInline>{"X"}</MathInline> contains the features, <MathInline>{"\\beta"}</MathInline> the coefficients, and <MathInline>{"\\varepsilon"}</MathInline> the error term.
</MdxCard>

## Intuition

<MdxCard>
Linear regression searches for the **best hyperplane** approximating a continuous target from input variables. The standard criterion is the residual sum of squares.

In the simple case:

<MathBlock>{"y_i = \\beta_0 + \\beta_1 x_i + \\varepsilon_i"}</MathBlock>

The coefficient <MathInline>{"\\beta_1"}</MathInline> measures the average change in <MathInline>{"y"}</MathInline> associated with a one-unit increase in <MathInline>{"x"}</MathInline>, holding other variables fixed in the multiple case.
</MdxCard>

## Ordinary least squares estimation

<MdxCard>
The OLS estimator minimizes:

<MathBlock>{"\\min_{\\beta} \\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\\top \\beta)^2"}</MathBlock>

In matrix form, if <MathInline>{"X^\\top X"}</MathInline> is invertible:

<MathBlock>{"\\hat{\\beta} = (X^\\top X)^{-1} X^\\top y"}</MathBlock>

This formula immediately shows why collinearity is problematic: if the columns of <MathInline>{"X"}</MathInline> are linearly dependent, the inverse does not exist.
</MdxCard>

## Theoretical properties

<MdxCard>
Under the standard assumptions, the OLS estimator is:

- **unbiased**: <MathInline>{"\\mathbb{E}[\\hat{\\beta} \\mid X] = \\beta"}</MathInline>
- **consistent** as sample size increases under regularity conditions
- **BLUE** (Best Linear Unbiased Estimator) by the Gauss-Markov theorem

Its conditional variance is:

<MathBlock>{"\\mathrm{Var}(\\hat{\\beta} \\mid X) = \\sigma^2 (X^\\top X)^{-1}"}</MathBlock>

Under Gaussian errors, OLS also coincides with the maximum likelihood estimator.
</MdxCard>

p-values are not enough to judge a model. You should also look at:

- coefficient magnitude and sign
- out-of-sample stability
- business or economic coherence
- sensitivity to variables being added or removed

## Limits

<MdxCard>
- Causality does not follow from a significant linear correlation.
- Outliers can strongly move coefficients.
- The true relationship may be nonlinear or depend on omitted interactions.
- With leakage or wrong temporal ordering, inference becomes misleading.
- Linear regression extrapolates as a line or hyperplane, which can be economically absurd outside the observed region.
</MdxCard>

## Linear regression as an ML baseline

<MdxCard>
In tabular machine learning, linear regression remains an **essential baseline**:

- fast to train
- highly interpretable
- strong comparison point before nonlinear models
- useful to detect whether extra complexity (trees, boosting, neural nets) really adds value

It is especially relevant when data are scarce, noise is moderate, and coefficient interpretation matters.
</MdxCard>
