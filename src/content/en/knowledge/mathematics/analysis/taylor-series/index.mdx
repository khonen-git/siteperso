# Taylor series

<MdxCard title="Definition">
Let <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline> be <MathInline>{"n"}</MathInline> times differentiable near <MathInline>{"a"}</MathInline>. The **Taylor polynomial of order <MathInline>n</MathInline>** is:

<MathBlock>{"T_n(x) = \\sum_{k=0}^{n} \\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k"}</MathBlock>

At <MathInline>{"a = 0"}</MathInline>, this is a **Maclaurin expansion**. If the series converges to <MathInline>{"f(x)"}</MathInline>:

<MathBlock>{"f(x) = \\sum_{k=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k"}</MathBlock>

this is the **Taylor series** of <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline> around <MathInline>{"a"}</MathInline>.
</MdxCard>

## Intuition

<MdxCard>
A Taylor series replaces a complicated function **locally** with a polynomial whose coefficients are fixed by derivatives at a point.

- Order 0: constant value <MathInline>{"f(a)"}</MathInline>.
- Order 1: **linear** approximation (tangent) — see [Derivatives, gradient, and Hessian](/knowledge/mathematics/analysis/derivatives-gradient-hessian).
- Order 2: **quadratic** approximation (curvature via the Hessian).
- Higher order: better accuracy **near <MathInline>{"a"}</MathInline>**, but often useless or unstable far from <MathInline>{"a"}</MathInline>.

Central idea: any sufficiently smooth function behaves almost like a polynomial at small scale.
</MdxCard>

## Univariate and multivariate cases

<MdxCard title="One dimension">
Expansion around <MathInline>{"a"}</MathInline> up to order 2:

<MathBlock>{"f(x) \\approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \\frac{f''(a)}{2}(x-a)^2"}</MathBlock>

The linear term dominates for small <MathInline>{"|x-a|"}</MathInline>; the quadratic term captures curvature.
</MdxCard>

<MdxCard title="Several variables">
For <MathInline>{"f : \\mathbb{R}^n \\to \\mathbb{R}"}</MathInline> near <MathInline>{"x_0"}</MathInline>:

<MathBlock>{"f(x) \\approx f(x_0) + \\nabla f(x_0)^\\top (x - x_0) + \\frac{1}{2}(x - x_0)^\\top \\nabla^2 f(x_0)(x - x_0)"}</MathBlock>

This is the order-2 Taylor expansion — foundation of Newton's method and delta-gamma approximations in finance.
</MdxCard>

## Remainder and accuracy

<MdxCard title="Lagrange form (order n)">
Between <MathInline>{"a"}</MathInline> and <MathInline>{"x"}</MathInline>, there exists <MathInline>{"\\xi"}</MathInline> such that:

<MathBlock>{"f(x) = T_n(x) + \\frac{f^{(n+1)}(\\xi)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1}"}</MathBlock>

The remainder depends on the <MathInline>{"(n+1)"}</MathInline>-th derivative on the interval — hence the importance of regularity.
</MdxCard>

<MdxCard title="Asymptotic notation">
We often write:

<MathBlock>{"f(x) = T_n(x) + o\\left((x-a)^n\\right) \\quad \\text{as } x \\to a"}</MathBlock>

In practice: truncating at order 1 or 2 suffices for optimization and sensitivities; higher orders serve mainly in numerical analysis or statistical asymptotics.
</MdxCard>

## Applications

<MdxCard>
**Optimization**

- **Newton's method**: solve <MathInline>{"\\nabla f(x) = 0"}</MathInline> by linearizing the gradient (order-1 Taylor on <MathInline>{"\\nabla f"}</MathInline>).
- **Convergence analysis**: quadratic rate of Newton near a simple root.

**Finance**

- **Delta-gamma**: portfolio options P&amp;L approximation:
  <MathInline>{"\\Delta P \\approx \\Delta \\cdot \\Delta S + \\frac{1}{2}\\Gamma \\cdot (\\Delta S)^2"}</MathInline> — order-2 Taylor of price.
- **Duration-convexity** (bonds): same logic for price vs yield.

**Statistics**

- Taylor expansion around the MLE to approximate likelihood and derive tests (normal asymptotics).
- Approximation <MathInline>{"\\log(1+x) \\approx x"}</MathInline> for small returns.

**Numerical methods**

- Integration, root finding, finite differences — see [Numerical methods](/knowledge/mathematics/numerical-methods) (coming soon).
</MdxCard>
