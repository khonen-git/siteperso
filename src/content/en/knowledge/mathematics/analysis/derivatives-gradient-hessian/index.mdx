# Derivatives, gradient, and Hessian

<MdxCard title="Definition">
For a differentiable function <MathInline>{"f : \\mathbb{R}^n \\to \\mathbb{R}"}</MathInline>, the **gradient** is the vector of partial derivatives:

<MathBlock>{"\\nabla f(x) = \\left(\\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial x_1}, \\ldots, \\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial x_n}\\right)^\\top"}</MathBlock>

The **Hessian** is the matrix of second derivatives:

<MathBlock>{"\\nabla^2 f(x) = \\left[ \\frac{\\partial^2 f}{\\partial x_i \\partial x_j} \\right]_{i,j}"}</MathBlock>

In one dimension, the derivative <MathInline>{"f'(x)"}</MathInline> generalizes the instantaneous rate of change; the gradient and Hessian extend this idea to multivariate functions.
</MdxCard>

## Intuition

<MdxCard>
- **Derivative / gradient**: local direction and rate of change. <MathInline>{"\\nabla f(x)"}</MathInline> points toward the steepest **increase** of <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline>; <MathInline>{"-\\nabla f(x)"}</MathInline> indicates the steepest **descent**.
- **Hessian**: local curvature. Its eigenvalues describe whether the function is more concave or convex along each direction.
- **Linear approximation**: near <MathInline>{"x_0"}</MathInline>,

<MathBlock>{"f(x) \\approx f(x_0) + \\nabla f(x_0)^\\top (x - x_0)"}</MathBlock>

- **Quadratic approximation**: adding curvature,

<MathBlock>{"f(x) \\approx f(x_0) + \\nabla f(x_0)^\\top (x - x_0) + \\frac{1}{2}(x - x_0)^\\top \\nabla^2 f(x_0)(x - x_0)"}</MathBlock>

This is the foundation of [gradient descent](/knowledge/mathematics/optimization/gradient-descent), Newton's method, and [Taylor series](/knowledge/mathematics/analysis/taylor-series).
</MdxCard>

## Link with convexity

<MdxCard>
For a twice-differentiable function:

- <MathInline>{"\\nabla^2 f(x) \\succ 0"}</MathInline> (positive definite) ⟹ <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline> is strictly convex locally;
- <MathInline>{"\\nabla^2 f(x) \\succeq 0"}</MathInline> everywhere ⟹ <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline> is convex;
- a point where <MathInline>{"\\nabla f(x) = 0"}</MathInline> and <MathInline>{"\\nabla^2 f(x) \\succ 0"}</MathInline> is a **local minimum**; on a convex domain, it is the global minimum.

See [Convexity](/knowledge/mathematics/analysis/convexity) for the full characterizations (Jensen, gradient criterion).
</MdxCard>

## Applications

<MdxCard>
**Optimization**

- **Gradient descent**: update <MathInline>{"x_{k+1} = x_k - \\eta \\nabla f(x_k)"}</MathInline> — see [Gradient descent](/knowledge/mathematics/optimization/gradient-descent).
- **Newton's method**: uses <MathInline>{"\\nabla^2 f"}</MathInline> to correct direction and converge faster near the minimum.
- **Optimality conditions**: without constraints, an interior optimum satisfies <MathInline>{"\\nabla f(x^\\star) = 0"}</MathInline>.

**Statistics and ML**

- **[Linear regression](/knowledge/statistics/regression)**: setting the quadratic loss gradient to zero yields the OLS estimator.
- **L2 regularization**: adds <MathInline>{"\\lambda I"}</MathInline> to the Hessian, improving conditioning.
- **Backpropagation**: gradient propagation via the chain rule on a computation graph.

**Finance**

- **Greeks** (delta, gamma) are derivatives of a derivative price with respect to risk factors — the same differential logic applied to pricing.
</MdxCard>
