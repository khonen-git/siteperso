# Convex optimization

<MdxCard title="Definition">
A **convex optimization problem** minimizes a convex objective over a **convex set**, possibly with convex constraints:

<MathBlock>{"\\min_{x \\in \\mathcal{C}} f(x) \\quad \\text{subject to} \\quad g_i(x) \\leq 0,\\; h_j(x) = 0"}</MathBlock>

where <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline> and the <MathInline>{"g_i"}</MathInline> are convex, the <MathInline>{"h_j"}</MathInline> are **affine**, and <MathInline>{"\\mathcal{C}"}</MathInline> is a convex set (often <MathInline>{"\\mathbb{R}^n"}</MathInline> or a subspace).

The most common practical form:

<MathBlock>{"\\min_x f(x) \\quad \\text{subject to} \\quad Ax = b,\\; Gx \\preceq h"}</MathBlock>

with <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline> convex and linear constraints.
</MdxCard>

## Intuition

<MdxCard>
Convex optimization is the "well-posed" case of optimization: there are no trapped local minima. If you find a point where the gradient vanishes (unconstrained) or KKT conditions hold (constrained), you have the **global minimum**.

This is why problems as diverse as least squares, ridge regularization, mean-variance allocation, and SVMs rely on this structure: reliable, often unique solutions with proven algorithms.
</MdxCard>

## Why convexity is enough

<MdxCard>
Prerequisites (see [Convexity](/knowledge/mathematics/analysis/convexity) and [Derivatives, gradient, and Hessian](/knowledge/mathematics/analysis/derivatives-gradient-hessian)):

- intersection of convex constraints = convex domain;
- sum of convex functions = convex objective;
- every local minimum of a convex function on a convex domain is **global**.

Practical consequence: no aggressive multi-start or escape heuristics needed to avoid local minima — difficulty comes mainly from **scale**, **conditioning**, and **computational cost**.
</MdxCard>

## Optimality conditions

<MdxCard title="Unconstrained case">
If <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline> is convex and differentiable, a point <MathInline>{"x^\\star"}</MathInline> is optimal if and only if:

<MathBlock>{"\\nabla f(x^\\star) = 0"}</MathBlock>
</MdxCard>

<MdxCard title="KKT conditions (constrained)">
For a convex problem with qualified constraints, Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions are **necessary and sufficient**:

<MathBlock>{"\\nabla f(x^\\star) + \\sum_i \\lambda_i \\nabla g_i(x^\\star) + \\sum_j \\mu_j \\nabla h_j(x^\\star) = 0"}</MathBlock>

<MathBlock>{"g_i(x^\\star) \\leq 0,\\; h_j(x^\\star) = 0,\\; \\lambda_i \\geq 0,\\; \\lambda_i g_i(x^\\star) = 0"}</MathBlock>

Multipliers <MathInline>{"\\lambda_i"}</MathInline> measure the **marginal cost** of a constraint: slightly relaxing an active constraint shifts the optimum by roughly <MathInline>{"\\lambda_i"}</MathInline>.
</MdxCard>

<MdxCard title="Lagrange multipliers">
With equality constraints only (<MathInline>{"h(x)=0"}</MathInline>), form the Lagrangian:

<MathBlock>{"\\mathcal{L}(x, \\mu) = f(x) + \\mu^\\top h(x)"}</MathBlock>

The optimum satisfies <MathInline>{"\\nabla_x \\mathcal{L} = 0"}</MathInline> and <MathInline>{"h(x)=0"}</MathInline>. With inequalities, Lagrange multipliers generalize via KKT.
</MdxCard>

## Duality

<MdxCard>
Every convex problem (primal) has an associated **dual problem**, often simpler to optimize or interpret:

- the dual provides a **lower bound** on the primal optimum (weak duality always holds);
- under **strong duality** (Slater, constraint qualification), primal optimum = dual optimum;
- dual multipliers are the dual problem's variables.

Classic example: in SVM, the dual turns a constrained problem into maximization over multipliers <MathInline>{"\\alpha_i"}</MathInline>, with a sparse solution (support vectors).
</MdxCard>

## Applications

<MdxCard>
- **Statistics**: MLE with convex constraints, ridge/lasso, quantile regression (via LP reformulation).
- **Machine learning**: SVM, logistic regression (convex in <MathInline>{"\\beta"}</MathInline>), regularization.
- **Finance**: efficient frontier, risk parity under linear constraints, robust optimization.
- **Engineering**: resource allocation, convex discrete control, constrained calibration.

See [Gradient descent](/knowledge/mathematics/optimization/gradient-descent) for algorithm details; Newton and quasi-Newton remain on the [Optimization](/knowledge/mathematics/optimization) hub.
</MdxCard>
