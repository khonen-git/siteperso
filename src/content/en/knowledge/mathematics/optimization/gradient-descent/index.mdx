# Gradient descent

<MdxCard title="Definition">
**Gradient descent** minimizes a differentiable function <MathInline>{"f : \\mathbb{R}^n \\to \\mathbb{R}"}</MathInline> through successive steps opposite to the gradient:

<MathBlock>{"x_{k+1} = x_k - \\eta_k \\nabla f(x_k)"}</MathBlock>

where <MathInline>{"\\eta_k > 0"}</MathInline> is the **learning rate** at iteration <MathInline>{"k"}</MathInline>.

The idea: the gradient points toward steepest ascent; subtracting <MathInline>{"\\nabla f"}</MathInline> moves locally toward a minimum.
</MdxCard>

## Intuition

<MdxCard>
Imagine a ball rolling on a surface: at each step, read the local slope (<MathInline>{"\\nabla f"}</MathInline>) and move in the steepest descent direction.

- Step **too large**: overshoot and divergence.
- Step **too small**: very slow convergence.
- On a **convex** smooth function, a good choice of <MathInline>{"\\eta"}</MathInline> guarantees convergence to the global minimum (see [Convex optimization](/knowledge/mathematics/optimization/convex-optimization)).
- On a **non-convex** function (neural networks), you often land at a local minimum or saddle — hence initialization and variants (momentum, Adam).
</MdxCard>

For an <MathInline>{"L"}</MathInline>-smooth convex function, a constant step <MathInline>{"\\eta \\leq 1/L"}</MathInline> guarantees convergence. In practice, <MathInline>{"L"}</MathInline> is rarely known: test values (log-scale: <MathInline>{"10^{-4}"}</MathInline>, <MathInline>{"10^{-3}"}</MathInline>, …) or use an adaptive optimizer.
</MdxCard>

## Convergence

<MdxCard title="Smooth convex case">
If <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline> is convex and <MathInline>{"L"}</MathInline>-smooth, gradient descent with <MathInline>{"\\eta = 1/L"}</MathInline> satisfies:

<MathBlock>{"f(x_k) - f(x^\\star) \\leq \\frac{L \\|x_0 - x^\\star\\|^2}{2k}"}</MathBlock>

<MathInline>{"O(1/k)"}</MathInline> convergence — linear in iteration count for **accuracy**, not exponential. For faster convergence near the optimum, see Newton and quasi-Newton (coming soon).
</MdxCard>

<MdxCard title="Strongly convex case">
If <MathInline>{"f"}</MathInline> is <MathInline>{"\\mu"}</MathInline>-strongly convex (<MathInline>{"\\nabla^2 f \\succeq \\mu I"}</MathInline>), the rate becomes **linear** (geometric):

<MathBlock>{"f(x_k) - f(x^\\star) \\leq \\left(1 - \\frac{\\mu}{L}\\right)^k (f(x_0) - f(x^\\star))"}</MathBlock>

The condition number <MathInline>{"L/\\mu"}</MathInline> governs speed: the larger it is, the slower the convergence.
</MdxCard>

## Applications

<MdxCard>
- **[Linear regression](/knowledge/statistics/regression)**: OLS has a closed form, but gradient descent remains useful for large <MathInline>{"n"}</MathInline> or streaming.
- **Ridge / lasso regularization**: iterative minimization when <MathInline>{"n"}</MathInline> or <MathInline>{"p"}</MathInline> is very large.
- **Machine learning**: training linear models, neural networks (via backprop + SGD/Adam).
- **Calibration**: fit model parameters by minimizing a loss on historical data.

Gradient descent is the baseline algorithm in [Derivatives, gradient, and Hessian](/knowledge/mathematics/analysis/derivatives-gradient-hessian); [Convex optimization](/knowledge/mathematics/optimization/convex-optimization) explains why, on a convex problem, this procedure targets the right optimum.
</MdxCard>
